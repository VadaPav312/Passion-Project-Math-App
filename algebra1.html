N-Q.1

Using Units and Dimensional Analysis

Use units as a way to understand problems and to guide the solution of multi-step problems; choose and interpret units consistently in formulas; choose and interpret the scale and the origin in graphs and data displays.

Key Concepts

  • Units carry meaning — they tell you what is being measured
  • Dimensional analysis: multiply by conversion factors (fractions equal to 1) to change units
  • Units must be consistent within a formula (e.g., both distances in meters)
  • The scale and origin chosen for a graph affect how data appears

Example

Convert 60 miles per hour to feet per second.

60 mihr × 5280 ft1 mi × 1 hr3600 s = 88 ft/s

Cancel units like variables: miles cancel, hours cancel, leaving ft/s.

N-Q.2

Defining Appropriate Quantities

Define appropriate quantities for the purpose of descriptive modeling.

Key Concepts

  • Choose quantities that directly represent what the problem is asking about
  • Label variables clearly with units (e.g., let t = time in hours)
  • Sometimes the best quantity is a ratio or rate (e.g., cost per item)
  • Derived quantities: computed from others (e.g., area = length × width)

Example

A store sells apples at $1.20 each and oranges at $0.80 each. You buy a apples and r oranges. Total cost C = 1.20a + 0.80r dollars. Here C, a, and r are the appropriate quantities.

N-Q.3

Accuracy and Precision in Measurement

Choose a level of accuracy appropriate to limitations on measurement when reporting quantities.

Key Concepts

  • Precision: how fine the measurement tool is (decimal places)
  • Accuracy: how close to the true value a measurement is
  • Significant figures reflect the precision of the original measurement
  • Calculated results should not imply more precision than inputs allow

Example

A ruler measures to the nearest mm. A board is measured as 1.235 m. Reporting the board as 1.23500000 m implies false precision. "1.235 m" or "1.24 m" is appropriate.

A-SSE.1

Interpreting Expressions

Interpret parts of an expression, such as terms, factors, and coefficients, in context. Interpret complicated expressions by viewing one or more of their parts as a single entity.

Key Concepts

  • Term: a single number, variable, or product (e.g., 3x², −5y)
  • Coefficient: the numerical factor of a term (e.g., 3 in 3x²)
  • Factor: something multiplied (e.g., in 6x, factors are 6 and x)
  • In context: P(1 + r)n — P is principal, r is interest rate, n is periods

Example

In the expression 4πr², identify the parts: 4 is the coefficient, π is a constant, r is the variable, and the whole expression represents the surface area related to a sphere's radius r.

A-SSE.2

Rewriting Expressions Using Structure

Use the structure of an expression to identify ways to rewrite it.

Key Concepts

  • Recognize patterns like difference of squares: a² − b² = (a+b)(a−b)
  • Factor out GCF before applying other factoring methods
  • Recognize perfect square trinomials: a² + 2ab + b² = (a+b)²
  • Group terms to factor: ax + ay + bx + by = a(x+y) + b(x+y) = (a+b)(x+y)

Example

Rewrite x⁴ − 16 by viewing it as (x²)² − 4² — a difference of squares.

(x²)² − 4² = (x² + 4)(x² − 4) = (x² + 4)(x + 2)(x − 2)

A-SSE.3

Equivalent Forms of Expressions

Choose and produce an equivalent form of an expression to reveal and explain properties of the quantity represented by the expression. Factor a quadratic to reveal its zeros; complete the square to reveal the maximum or minimum; use properties of exponents to rewrite exponential expressions.

Key Concepts

  • Factored form reveals zeros: x² − 5x + 6 = (x−2)(x−3) → zeros at x = 2, 3
  • Vertex form (completing the square) reveals max/min: a(x−h)² + k, vertex at (h, k)
  • Standard form: ax² + bx + c reveals y-intercept directly
  • Exponential: 2 · 4t = 2 · (2²)t = 2 · 22t = 22t+1

Example

Complete the square for x² + 6x + 2:

x² + 6x + 9 − 9 + 2 = (x + 3)² − 7 → vertex at (−3, −7), which is the minimum.

A-APR.1

Polynomial Operations

Understand that polynomials form a system analogous to the integers, namely, they are closed under the operations of addition, subtraction, and multiplication; add, subtract, and multiply polynomials.

Key Concepts

  • Closed: adding, subtracting, or multiplying polynomials always yields a polynomial
  • Add/Subtract: combine like terms (same variable and exponent)
  • Multiply: use distributive property (FOIL for two binomials)
  • Degree of a product = sum of degrees of factors

Example

Multiply (2x + 3)(x − 5):

= 2x · x + 2x · (−5) + 3 · x + 3 · (−5)

= 2x² − 10x + 3x − 15 = 2x² − 7x − 15

A-CED.1

Equations and Inequalities in One Variable

Create equations and inequalities in one variable and use them to solve problems. Include equations arising from linear and quadratic functions, and simple rational and exponential functions.

Key Concepts

  • Translate word problems into algebraic equations or inequalities
  • Linear: ax + b = c or ax + b < c
  • Quadratic: ax² + bx + c = 0
  • Exponential: 2x = 32 → x = 5
  • Rational: x3 + 1 = 5 → x = 12

Example

A phone plan costs $30/month plus $0.10/text. Model and solve: for how many texts does the bill equal $50?

30 + 0.10t = 50 → 0.10t = 20 → t = 200 texts

A-CED.2

Equations in Two Variables

Create equations in two or more variables to represent relationships between quantities; graph equations on coordinate axes with labels and scales.

Key Concepts

  • Two-variable equations describe a relationship between two quantities
  • The graph is the complete set of all (x, y) pairs satisfying the equation
  • Label axes with quantity names and units
  • Choose scale so important features are visible

Example

Water drains from a 100-gallon tank at 4 gallons per minute. Write and graph the equation.

W = 100 − 4t, where W = gallons remaining and t = minutes. Graph on axes labeled "t (min)" and "W (gal)". x-intercept at t = 25, y-intercept at W = 100.

A-CED.3

Constraints and Systems

Represent constraints by equations or inequalities, and by systems of equations and/or inequalities, and interpret solutions as viable or non-viable options in a modeling context.

Key Concepts

  • A constraint limits possible values (e.g., a budget, a physical limit)
  • A system captures multiple simultaneous constraints
  • Solutions must be checked against context — non-negative quantities, integers only, etc.
  • Infeasible solutions are non-viable

Example

A student can spend at most $20. Sandwiches cost $5, drinks cost $2. Write constraints: 5s + 2d ≤ 20, s ≥ 0, d ≥ 0. If s = 3, d = 2: 15 + 4 = 19 ≤ 20 ✓ (viable). If s = 5: cost = 25 > 20 (non-viable).

A-CED.4

Rearranging Formulas

Rearrange formulas to highlight a quantity of interest, using the same reasoning as in solving equations.

Key Concepts

  • Use inverse operations just as in solving equations, but treat other variables as constants
  • Goal: isolate the target variable on one side
  • Common formulas: d = rt, A = lw, V = lwh, C = 2πr, A = πr²

Example

Solve A = 12bh for h:

Multiply both sides by 2: 2A = bh

Divide both sides by b: h = 2Ab

A-REI.1

Properties of Equality

Explain each step in solving a simple equation as following from the equality of numbers asserted at the previous step, starting from the assumption that the original equation has a solution.

Key Concepts

  • Addition/Subtraction Property: add or subtract equal amounts from both sides
  • Multiplication/Division Property: multiply or divide both sides by same nonzero number
  • Each step produces an equivalent equation (same solution set)
  • Justify steps: "add 3 to both sides," "divide both sides by 2," etc.

Example

Solve 3x − 7 = 8 with justification:

3x − 7 = 8     (given)

3x = 15        (add 7 to both sides)

x = 5           (divide both sides by 3)

A-REI.3

Linear Equations and Inequalities in One Variable

Solve linear equations and inequalities in one variable, including equations with coefficients represented by letters.

Key Concepts

  • Isolate the variable using inverse operations
  • Distribute and combine like terms first
  • Inequalities: flip the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative
  • Graph solutions on a number line; open circle for strict, closed for ≤ or ≥

Example

Solve −2x + 5 > 11:

−2x > 6     (subtract 5)

x < −3      (divide by −2, flip sign)

Solution: x < −3, graphed as an open circle at −3 pointing left.

A-REI.4

Solving Quadratic Equations

Solve quadratic equations in one variable by inspection, taking square roots, completing the square, the quadratic formula, and factoring, as appropriate to the initial form of the equation.

Key Concepts

  • Factoring: ax² + bx + c = 0 → (x − r₁)(x − r₂) = 0 → x = r₁ or r₂
  • Square root: x² = k → x = ±√k
  • Completing the square: add (b/2)² to both sides
  • Quadratic formula: x = −b ± b² − 4ac2a
  • The discriminant b² − 4ac determines number of real solutions: >0 two, =0 one, <0 none

Example

Solve x² − 5x + 6 = 0 by factoring:

(x − 2)(x − 3) = 0 → x = 2 or x = 3

A-REI.5

Equivalent Systems (Elimination Justification)

Prove that, given a system of two equations in two variables, replacing one equation by the sum of that equation and a multiple of the other produces a system with the same solutions.

Key Concepts

  • The elimination method is valid because adding a true equation to another preserves equality
  • If (x, y) satisfies both equations, it satisfies any linear combination of them
  • The new system has the same solution set as the original

Example

System: x + y = 5 and 2x − y = 1. Adding them: 3x = 6, so x = 2. Since (2,3) satisfies both originals, it's valid — the combined equation preserved the solution.

A-REI.6

Solving Systems of Linear Equations

Solve systems of linear equations exactly and approximately (e.g., with graphs), focusing on pairs of linear equations in two variables.

Key Concepts

  • Substitution: solve one equation for a variable, substitute into the other
  • Elimination: add or subtract multiples of equations to cancel a variable
  • Graphing: solution is the intersection point of the two lines
  • No solution (parallel lines) or infinite solutions (same line)

Example

Solve: y = 2x + 1 and y = −x + 7 (substitution):

2x + 1 = −x + 7 → 3x = 6 → x = 2, y = 5. Solution: (2, 5)

A-REI.10

Graphs as Solution Sets

Understand that the graph of an equation in two variables is the set of all its solutions plotted in the coordinate plane, often forming a curve (which could be a line).

Key Concepts

  • Every point (x, y) on the graph satisfies the equation
  • Points not on the graph do not satisfy the equation
  • The graph is a visual representation of infinitely many solutions

Example

For y = x² − 1: does (3, 8) lie on the graph? Check: 3² − 1 = 9 − 1 = 8 ✓ Yes. Does (2, 1)? 2² − 1 = 3 ≠ 1. No.

A-REI.11

Intersection Points as Solutions

Explain why the x-coordinates of the points where the graphs of y = f(x) and y = g(x) intersect are the solutions of the equation f(x) = g(x); find the solutions approximately, e.g., using technology to graph the functions.

Key Concepts

  • At intersection: both functions give the same y, so f(x) = g(x)
  • Solving f(x) = g(x) algebraically finds x-coordinates of intersections
  • Graphically, intersections show where two quantities are equal

Example

Find where 2x + 1 = x² − 3: rearrange to x² − 2x − 4 = 0. Quadratic formula gives x ≈ 3.24 and x ≈ −1.24. These are the x-coordinates of intersection.

A-REI.12

Graphing Linear Inequalities

Graph the solutions to a linear inequality in two variables as a half-plane (excluding the boundary in the case of a strict inequality), and graph the solution set of a system of linear inequalities as the intersection of the corresponding half-planes.

Key Concepts

  • Graph the boundary line first (dashed for strict < or >, solid for ≤ or ≥)
  • Test a point (like the origin) to determine which side is shaded
  • For systems: the solution is the overlapping (intersection) region of all shaded areas

Example

Graph y > 2x − 1: draw a dashed line y = 2x − 1. Test (0,0): 0 > −1 ✓, so shade above the line.

F-IF.1

Function Definition and Domain/Range

Understand that a function from one set (called the domain) to another set (called the range) assigns to each element of the domain exactly one element of the range. If f is a function and x is an element of its domain, then f(x) denotes the output. The graph of f is the graph of the equation y = f(x).

Key Concepts

  • Each input has exactly one output (vertical line test on a graph)
  • Domain: all allowable inputs (x-values)
  • Range: all corresponding outputs (y-values)
  • A relation is a function if no x-value is repeated with different y-values

Example

{(1,2), (2,4), (3,6)} — is this a function? Yes, each input (1, 2, 3) maps to exactly one output. {(1,2), (1,3)} is not a function — input 1 maps to two outputs.

F-IF.2

Function Notation and Evaluation

Use function notation, evaluate functions for inputs in their domains, and interpret statements that use function notation in terms of a context.

Key Concepts

  • f(x) means "the output of function f when the input is x"
  • Evaluate by substituting the input value for x
  • f(a) + f(b) ≠ f(a + b) in general
  • f(x + h) means substitute (x + h) everywhere x appears

Example

If f(x) = 3x² − 2x + 1, find f(−2):

f(−2) = 3(−2)² − 2(−2) + 1 = 3(4) + 4 + 1 = 12 + 4 + 1 = 17

F-IF.3

Sequences as Functions

Recognize that sequences are functions, sometimes defined recursively, whose domain is a subset of the integers.

Key Concepts

  • A sequence is a list of numbers where position n is the input
  • Explicit formula: directly computes the nth term (e.g., an = 2n + 1)
  • Recursive formula: each term defined using previous term (e.g., an = an−1 + 3)
  • Domain is typically {1, 2, 3, …} (positive integers)

Example

Sequence: 3, 7, 11, 15, … Explicit: an = 4n − 1. Recursive: a1 = 3, an = an−1 + 4. Both define the same function on positive integers.

F-IF.4

Key Features of Functions and Graphs

For a function that models a relationship between two quantities, interpret key features of graphs and tables in terms of the quantities, and sketch graphs showing key features given a verbal description.

Key Concepts

  • Intercepts: where the graph crosses the axes
  • Intervals of increase/decrease: where the graph rises/falls left to right
  • Maximum/minimum: peak (local/global) or valley values
  • Symmetry: even functions symmetric over y-axis; odd functions over origin
  • End behavior: what happens as x → ±∞
  • Positive/negative: where f(x) > 0 or f(x) < 0

Example

For f(x) = −x² + 4: intercepts at (±2, 0) and (0, 4); maximum at (0, 4); decreasing for x > 0, increasing for x < 0; end behavior → −∞ as x → ±∞.

F-IF.5

Domain in Context

Relate the domain of a function to its graph and, where applicable, to the quantitative relationship it describes.

Key Concepts

  • Mathematical domain may be all reals, but context restricts it
  • Time can't be negative; counts must be whole numbers; physical measurements have upper limits
  • Identify if the domain is discrete or continuous

Example

A function models the height of a ball thrown upward: h(t) = −16t² + 64t. Mathematically defined for all t, but context requires 0 ≤ t ≤ 4 (the ball is in the air for 4 seconds). Domain = [0, 4].

F-IF.6

Average Rate of Change

Calculate and interpret the average rate of change of a function (presented symbolically or as a table) over a specified interval. Estimate the rate of change from a graph.

Key Concepts

  • Average rate of change over [a, b] = f(b) − f(a)b − a
  • This equals the slope of the secant line connecting (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b))
  • For a linear function, average rate of change is constant (= slope)
  • Units: (units of output) per (unit of input)

Example

f(x) = x² over [1, 4]: f(4) − f(1)4 − 1 = 16 − 13 = 5. The function increases on average 5 units per unit of x over that interval.

F-IF.7

Graphing Functions

Graph functions expressed symbolically and show key features of the graph. Graph linear and quadratic functions; graph square root, cube root, and piecewise-defined functions; graph exponential and logarithmic functions.

Key Concepts

  • Linear: y = mx + b — slope and y-intercept
  • Quadratic: y = ax² + bx + c — vertex, axis of symmetry, direction of opening
  • Square root: y = √x — starts at (0,0), only defined for x ≥ 0
  • Absolute value: y = |x| — V-shape with vertex at origin
  • Exponential: y = abx — horizontal asymptote, always positive (if a > 0)
  • Piecewise: different formulas for different parts of the domain

Example

Graph f(x) = |x − 2| + 1: vertex at (2, 1), arms with slope ±1, V-shape opening upward.

F-IF.8

Writing Functions in Equivalent Forms

Write a function defined by an expression in different but equivalent forms to reveal and explain different properties of the function.

Key Concepts

  • Factored form: reveals zeros of quadratic
  • Vertex form: a(x−h)² + k reveals vertex (h, k)
  • Standard form: ax² + bx + c reveals y-intercept (c)
  • Exponential: factor to reveal growth rate and starting value

Example

f(x) = 2x² − 8x + 6: factor as 2(x−1)(x−3) → zeros at x=1,3. Vertex form: 2(x−2)² − 2 → vertex at (2, −2). Both forms are equivalent.

F-IF.9

Comparing Functions

Compare properties of two functions each represented in a different way (algebraically, graphically, numerically in tables, or by verbal descriptions).

Key Concepts

  • Extract comparable properties from different representations
  • Compare: slopes, y-intercepts, maxima/minima, zeros, rates of change
  • A function in a table can reveal patterns; a graph shows shape; algebra gives exact values

Example

Function A: graphed with y-intercept 3 and slope 2. Function B: y = 3x − 1. Which has the greater y-intercept? A does (3 > −1). Which grows faster? B does (slope 3 > 2).

F-BF.1

Writing Functions from Context

Write a function that describes a relationship between two quantities. Determine an explicit expression, a recursive process, or steps for calculation from a context. Combine standard function types using arithmetic operations.

Key Concepts

  • Identify the independent variable (input) and dependent variable (output)
  • Look for rates of change, initial values, and patterns in data
  • Functions can be added, subtracted, multiplied: (f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)

Example

A car travels at 55 mph. Write distance as a function of time: d(t) = 55t. If a second car starts 20 miles ahead: total lead = 20 − (55t − 60t) for the first car catching up is a combined function.

F-BF.2

Arithmetic and Geometric Sequences

Write arithmetic and geometric sequences both recursively and with an explicit formula, use them to model situations, and translate between the two forms.

Key Concepts

  • Arithmetic sequence: add constant d each time. Explicit: an = a1 + (n−1)d
  • Geometric sequence: multiply by constant r. Explicit: an = a1 · rn−1
  • Arithmetic: constant differences → linear function
  • Geometric: constant ratios → exponential function

Example

Geometric sequence: 3, 6, 12, 24, … → r = 2. Explicit: an = 3 · 2n−1. Recursive: a1 = 3, an = 2an−1.

F-BF.3

Transformations of Functions

Identify the effect on the graph of replacing f(x) by f(x) + k, k·f(x), f(kx), and f(x + k) for specific values of k (both positive and negative); find the value of k given the graphs.

Key Concepts

  • f(x) + k: vertical shift up k (or down if k < 0)
  • f(x + k): horizontal shift left k (right if k < 0)
  • k·f(x): vertical stretch (|k|>1) or compression (|k|<1); k<0 flips over x-axis
  • f(kx): horizontal compression (|k|>1) or stretch (|k|<1); k<0 flips over y-axis

Example

Starting from y = x²: shift right 3 → y = (x−3)²; shift up 2 → y = (x−3)² + 2; reflect over x-axis → y = −(x−3)² + 2. Vertex moves from (0,0) to (3,2).

F-BF.4

Inverse Functions

Find the inverse of a function. Solve an equation of the form f(x) = c for a simple function f that has an inverse and write an expression for the inverse.

Key Concepts

  • Inverse function f⁻¹ reverses the input/output of f: if f(a) = b, then f⁻¹(b) = a
  • To find algebraically: replace f(x) with y, swap x and y, solve for y
  • f and f⁻¹ are reflections over the line y = x
  • f(f⁻¹(x)) = x and f⁻¹(f(x)) = x

Example

Find the inverse of f(x) = 2x + 3: write y = 2x + 3, swap: x = 2y + 3, solve: y = x − 32. So f⁻¹(x) = x − 32.

F-LE.1

Linear vs. Exponential Growth

Distinguish between situations that can be modeled with linear functions and with exponential functions. Linear functions grow by equal differences; exponential functions grow by equal factors. Recognize situations with constant rate of change (linear) or constant percent rate of change (exponential).

Key Concepts

  • Linear: constant additive change (e.g., +5 each step)
  • Exponential: constant multiplicative change (e.g., ×2 each step)
  • Check differences (linear) or ratios (exponential) in a table
  • "Increases by $50/year" → linear; "increases by 5%/year" → exponential

Example

Table: n=1,2,3,4 → y=3,6,12,24. Ratios: 6/3=2, 12/6=2, 24/12=2. Constant ratio → exponential model y = 3 · 2n−1.

F-LE.2

Constructing Linear and Exponential Functions

Construct linear and exponential functions, including arithmetic and geometric sequences, given a graph, a description of a relationship, or two input-output pairs (include reading these from a table).

Key Concepts

  • Linear from two points: find slope m = y₂−y₁x₂−x₁, then b = y₁ − mx₁
  • Exponential from two points: set up a · bx = y for each point, divide to find b
  • From a graph: read the y-intercept and slope (linear) or y-intercept and growth/decay factor

Example

Points (0, 5) and (3, 40) on an exponential: a = 5 (from x=0), 5 · b³ = 40 → b³ = 8 → b = 2. Function: f(x) = 5 · 2x.

F-LE.3

Comparing Growth Rates

Observe, using graphs and tables, that a quantity increasing exponentially eventually exceeds a quantity increasing linearly, quadratically, or (more generally) as a polynomial function.

Key Concepts

  • Exponential functions eventually outpace any polynomial
  • For small x, polynomial may be larger; but exponential "wins" for large x
  • Example: 2x vs. x¹⁰⁰ — eventually 2x > x¹⁰⁰

Example

Compare f(x) = 1000x² and g(x) = 2x. At x=10: f=100000, g=1024. At x=30: f=900000, g≈10⁹. For large x, g overtakes and dominates f.

F-LE.5

Interpreting Parameters in Models

Interpret the parameters in a linear or exponential function in terms of a context.

Key Concepts

  • Linear f(x) = mx + b: m = rate of change (per unit x); b = starting value (at x=0)
  • Exponential f(x) = a · bx: a = initial amount; b = growth factor; b−1 = growth rate
  • Decay: b < 1, so 1−b is the decay rate

Example

Bank account: A(t) = 1000 · 1.04t. Parameters: $1000 is the initial deposit; 1.04 means 4% annual growth rate; A(t) is balance after t years.

S-ID.1

Data Representations

Represent data with plots on the real number line (dot plots, histograms, and box plots).

Key Concepts

  • Dot plot: each data value shown as a dot above a number line; shows individual values
  • Histogram: bars show frequency in intervals; good for large data sets
  • Box plot: shows five-number summary: min, Q1, median, Q3, max
  • Choose display based on data size and what features to highlight

Example

Data: 2, 3, 3, 5, 7, 8, 10. Five-number summary: min=2, Q1=3, median=5, Q3=8, max=10. Box spans Q1 to Q3 (3–8) with a line at median (5).

S-ID.2

Comparing Distributions

Use statistics appropriate to the shape of the data distribution to compare center (median, mean) and spread (interquartile range, standard deviation) of two or more different data sets.

Key Concepts

  • Mean: sum ÷ count; affected by outliers
  • Median: middle value; robust to outliers
  • IQR: Q3 − Q1; spread of middle 50%
  • Standard deviation: average distance from the mean
  • For skewed data or outliers, use median + IQR; for symmetric, use mean + SD

Example

Class A scores: median 78, IQR 15. Class B scores: median 82, IQR 30. Class B has a higher center but much more spread — scores are more variable.

S-ID.3

Shape, Center, and Spread

Interpret differences in shape, center, and spread in the context of the data sets, accounting for possible effects of extreme data points (outliers).

Key Concepts

  • Shape: symmetric, skewed left, skewed right, uniform, bimodal
  • Skewed right: tail on right → mean > median
  • Skewed left: tail on left → mean < median
  • Outlier: unusually far from rest; pulls mean toward it but not median

Example

Home prices: mostly $300k–$500k, but one $5M mansion. Distribution skewed right; mean much higher than median. Median better represents a "typical" home price.

S-ID.5

Two-Way Frequency Tables

Summarize categorical data for two categories in two-way frequency tables. Interpret relative frequencies in the context of the data (including joint, marginal, and conditional relative frequencies). Recognize possible associations and trends in the data.

Key Concepts

  • Joint frequency: count in a specific row AND column cell
  • Marginal frequency: row or column totals
  • Conditional relative frequency: fraction within a row or column (e.g., of males, what fraction plays sports?)
  • Look for association: if conditional frequencies differ by category, there may be an association

Example

Of 50 students: 15 boys play sports, 10 boys don't; 20 girls play sports, 5 don't. Conditional: 60% of boys play sports, 80% of girls play sports — girls more likely to play sports in this group.

S-ID.6

Scatter Plots and Lines of Best Fit

Represent data on two quantitative variables on a scatter plot, and describe how the variables are related. Fit a function to the data; use functions fitted to data to solve problems. Informally assess fit by plotting and analyzing residuals. Fit a linear function for scatter plots that suggest a linear association.

Key Concepts

  • Scatter plot: each point (x, y) represents one data item
  • Trend: positive (up-right), negative (down-right), or no association
  • Line of best fit (LSRL): minimizes sum of squared residuals
  • Residual: actual y − predicted y; random scatter around zero is a good sign
  • Use the model to predict: substitute an x-value into the equation

Example

Study hours vs. test scores: LSRL is ŷ = 5x + 60. Predicted score for 4 hours: 5(4)+60 = 80. Residual for a student who studied 4 hours and scored 75: 75 − 80 = −5.

S-ID.7

Interpreting Slope and Intercept in Context

Interpret the slope (rate of change) and the intercept (constant term) of a linear model in the context of the data.

Key Concepts

  • Slope = rate of change of y per unit increase in x (with units!)
  • Intercept = predicted y when x = 0 (may or may not be meaningful in context)
  • Always interpret using the actual variable names and units

Example

LSRL: ŷ = 5x + 60 where x = study hours, ŷ = predicted score. Slope: each additional study hour is associated with 5 more points. Intercept: a student who studies 0 hours is predicted to score 60.

S-ID.8

Correlation Coefficient

Compute (using technology) and interpret the correlation coefficient of a linear fit.

Key Concepts

  • r (correlation coefficient) ranges from −1 to 1
  • r close to ±1: strong linear association; r close to 0: weak/no linear association
  • Positive r: as x increases, y tends to increase
  • Negative r: as x increases, y tends to decrease
  • r² = coefficient of determination: percent of variation in y explained by x

Example

r = 0.92 for study hours vs. test score: strong positive linear association. r² = 0.846, meaning about 84.6% of variation in test scores is explained by study hours.

S-ID.9

Correlation vs. Causation

Distinguish between correlation and causation.

Key Concepts

  • Correlation: a statistical relationship between two variables
  • Causation: one variable directly causes a change in another
  • Correlation does not imply causation — a third variable (confounding variable) may explain both
  • Only a controlled experiment (randomized, with treatment/control groups) can establish causation

Example

Ice cream sales and drowning rates are positively correlated. Cause? Neither — warm weather (confounding variable) drives both. Banning ice cream would not reduce drownings.
